Slide 5: Problem Description & Analysis

"Now, let's dive into the core problem we faced and our analysis. On the left side, you can see the conventional display structure using PSPI (Positive Photosensitive Polyimide) as the pixel defining layer. On the right is our new approach with B-PDL (Black Pixel Define Layer). The fundamental difference lies in the chemical composition. PSPI is a positive photoresist with a binder polymer structure and PAC (Photoactive Compound) that becomes soluble when exposed to light. The B-PDL, however, is a negative photoresist containing black pigment in a mill base with photo initiator and monomer that undergoes radical polymerization during exposure. These material differences created unique challenges, particularly in terms of mechanical properties and adhesion characteristics, which we needed to solve for implementing stylus functionality in foldable displays."

Slide 6: Problem Phenomenon Description

"This slide illustrates the specific problem we encountered during scratch testing, which simulates stylus interaction with the display surface. The scratch test applies controlled force across the display surface, as shown in the left images. When pressure is applied, two primary failure modes occur: cracking due to compressive stress and adhesion failure at critical load points. The microscopic images highlight these defects in detail. On the right side, you can see how these failures manifest during actual pen usage scenarios - both when hitting the display and when writing on it. The diagram at the bottom right shows the mechanism of delamination that occurs under pressure, leading to dark spots and other visible defects in the display."

"Based on our analysis, we developed two key hypotheses. First, regarding modulus weakening for better pressure distribution. The simulation model on the left shows how pressure applied to the display surface creates tensile and compressive forces. The graph below compares the force-distance curves of PSPI (shown in blue) versus BPDL (shown in red), revealing their different mechanical responses. Our second hypothesis concerned adhesion. The right side shows tape test results comparing the adhesion performance of different materials. PSPI demonstrates excellent adhesion with the hole injection layer (HIL), while BPDL shows poor adhesion, resulting in delamination visible in the test images. The critical insight was that BPDL's hydrophobic character conflicted with the hydrophilic nature of the HIL, causing the poor adhesion. We needed to develop a solution that would maintain the optical benefits of BPDL while addressing these mechanical and adhesion challenges."

Slide 8: Breakthrough

"Our breakthrough came from an unexpected source of inspiration - sugar-coated donuts! The right side illustrates this half-tone spacer process. By strategically applying a thin layer of PSPI over the BPDL, we could leverage the excellent adhesion properties of PSPI while maintaining the optical benefits of the black layer. This innovative structure combines the best properties of both materials, solving the adhesion issues while preserving display performance."
